## Summary
Regulation Crowdfunding (Reg CF) allows US startups to raise up to $5M per year from both accredited AND non-accredited investors via SEC-registered funding portals. It is the democratized alternative to Reg D, enabling community-wide participation in token raises while requiring ongoing SEC disclosure.

## Key Facts
- **Raise cap**: $5M per 12-month period (as of 2021 increase from $1.07M)
- **Investor eligibility**: Both accredited and non-accredited investors
- **Non-accredited investor limits**: Based on annual income and net worth (lesser of the two applies)
- **No investment limits** for accredited investors
- Must use a **registered intermediary** (broker-dealer or FINRA-registered funding portal)
- Requires **financial disclosures** and ongoing SEC reporting
- Securities purchased generally **cannot be resold for one year**
- Republic.co uses Reg CF for its Token DPA (Deferred Purchase Agreement) product — enables non-accredited investors to buy rights to future tokens
- Republic's "Mirror Tokens" launched under Reg D (not Reg CF) in 2025 due to regulatory complexity

> [!fact] Confirmed from Republic.com and Crowdfund Insider (Jan 2026)
> Republic uses Token DPA under Reg CF for non-accredited investors. Republic Mirror Tokens pivoted to Reg D (accredited only) at launch.

> [!analysis] Reg CF is attractive for community-building token sales but the $5M cap limits institutional use. Hybrid Reg CF + Reg D stacking is common.

## How it relates to Logos
- Reg CF could enable broader community participation in Logos ecosystem token raises
- Lower barrier for retail investors; important for decentralization narratives
- The intermediary requirement means a third party always sits between issuer and investor — tension with permissionless ethos

## Refund & Participant Protection

> [!fact] Confirmed from SEC Rule 304(a) and 17 CFR Part 227 (Regulation Crowdfunding)

- **Refund window / cancellation rights:** Yes — **investors may cancel their investment commitment for any reason up to 48 hours before the offering deadline.** This is a mandatory SEC requirement under Rule 304(a). The issuer/portal must refund the investor's money upon cancellation.
- **Minimum raise threshold (soft cap):** Yes — Reg CF offerings must specify a target amount. **If the target is not met by the offering deadline, all investor commitments are cancelled and funds are returned.** This is a mandatory regulatory protection.
- **Post-launch performance refund:** No — once the offering closes successfully (target met, 48-hour window passed), securities are issued and there is no performance-based refund mechanism.
- **Material change re-opening:** Yes — if there is a **material change** to the offering terms, investors who have already committed must be given notice and their commitment is automatically cancelled unless they **reconfirm within 5 business days.**

> [!fact] SEC FAQ confirms: "Rule 304(a) requires that an investor be able to cancel an investment commitment for 'any reason' until 48 hours prior to the deadline identified in the issuer's offering materials."

> [!analysis] Reg CF provides the strongest mandatory participant protection of any token sale framework studied. The 48-hour cancellation window + mandatory minimum raise threshold + material change re-confirmation create a robust investor safety net. This is the closest regulatory equivalent to the "3-day refund period" concept raised in the user's question. Republic.co, as a registered funding portal, must enforce these protections for all its Reg CF raises.
